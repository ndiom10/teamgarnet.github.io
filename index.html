University of Virginia - CS 4501 AI & Humanity

Does AI help students learn, or make learning easier to avoid?

Team Garnet reviewed research on AI in education to ask whether generative tools simply shift skills, or whether overreliance causes deskilling through cognitive offloading, skill erosion, and the illusion of competence.

View Key Findings Read the Research

Research Question

AI is not just another calculator.

Earlier classroom tools mostly automated narrow tasks. AI can summarize, write, brainstorm, reason, and generate answers across subjects. That broader reach creates real educational benefits, but it also raises a harder question: what happens when students stop practicing the skills school is supposed to build?

10+sources reviewed across education, cognition, language, and AI policy
17.5%estimated growth of LLM-modified sentences in computer science papers
665participants in a study connecting AI use, offloading, and critical thinking
The core finding: AI can support learning when it scaffolds thinking, but it becomes risky when it replaces effort, verification, and independent problem-solving.

What We Found

Deskilling happens when convenience replaces practice.

Across the sources we reviewed, the same pattern appeared: moderate AI use can help students, but passive dependence can weaken the cognitive habits that produce real understanding.

1

Cognitive offloading can become overreliance

AI reduces mental load, but students lose practice when they let it handle the reasoning, memory, or problem-solving process for them.

2

The illusion of competence hides weak understanding

Students may produce polished work with AI support while still being unable to explain, defend, or recreate the thinking independently.

3

Writing and language are already shifting

Large-scale studies show rising LLM influence in scientific writing, suggesting AI can reshape not only individual assignments but broader writing norms.

4

Balanced policy matters more than simple bans

The best classroom approach preserves productive struggle while still allowing AI to serve as a tutor, study guide, accessibility tool, and feedback system.

Why It Matters

The risk is not one bad answer. It is a weaker learner.

Calculator comparison

Historical calculator studies generally found improved performance without clear damage to conceptual reasoning. AI is different because it can automate the exact skills education depends on: writing, evaluating, brainstorming, and explaining.

Calculator

Mostly automates computation

AI

Can automate broad cognitive work

Team Garnet position

AI should be used as a learning scaffold, not a shortcut. Students should think first, attempt first, verify outputs, and use AI to deepen understanding rather than avoid the difficult parts of learning.

In short: use AI second, not first.
