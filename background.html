Background

To understand AI’s role in education, it is important to examine the history of learning technologies and the concept of deskilling.

Historical Background

Schools have always adopted tools that improve efficiency, from pencils and textbooks to calculators and computers. Each innovation raised concerns that students might become too dependent on technology.

One of the clearest historical examples is the graphing calculator. During the 1990s, researchers studied whether calculators harmed students’ mathematical ability. Many studies found improved achievement with little evidence of reduced reasoning skills.

Additional SAT studies found students using calculators often performed better on computational tasks while showing similar performance on conceptual reasoning questions. This suggested calculators improved speed without replacing thinking.

Resistance to New Technology: Public concerns over calculators were once widespread. In 1988, teachers protested calculator use in elementary schools, arguing students would fail to learn core math concepts.

Today, artificial intelligence raises similar concerns—but with higher stakes. Unlike calculators, AI can generate writing, summarize information, solve problems, and imitate reasoning.

Defining Deskilling

Deskilling refers to the weakening or replacement of human skills through automation. In education, this may include reduced writing ability, weaker memory retention, or less persistence in solving problems independently.

Defining Cognitive Offloading

Cognitive offloading occurs when people rely on outside systems to perform mental tasks. Examples include GPS for navigation, search engines for recall, or AI tools for writing and summarizing.

Defining Skill Erosion

Skill erosion refers to the gradual decline of previously developed abilities through lack of use. If students repeatedly outsource thinking, writing, or problem-solving, those skills may weaken over time.
