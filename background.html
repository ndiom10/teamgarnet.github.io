Background

To understand AI’s role in education, it is important to examine the history of technology in learning and the concept of deskilling.

Historical Background

To understand AI in education, it helps to compare it with earlier learning technologies. Schools have always adopted tools that improve efficiency, from pencils and textbooks to calculators and computers. Each innovation sparked concern that students might become too dependent on technology.

One of the clearest historical examples is the graphing calculator. During the 1990s, researchers studied whether calculators harmed students' math ability. A major review found that more than two-thirds of studies showed higher achievement among calculator users, while most studies found no meaningful decline in procedural skills such as paper-and-pencil computation.

Additional studies on SAT mathematics testing found that students allowed calculators often performed better overall, especially on computational questions, while performing similarly on conceptual reasoning questions. This suggested calculators improved speed and efficiency without replacing core thinking skills.

Resistance to New Technology: Public concerns over calculators were once widespread. In 1988, teachers protested calculator use in elementary schools, arguing students would fail to learn core math concepts. The image reflects a recurring pattern: many new educational tools are first viewed as threats before their long-term effects are fully understood.

Today, artificial intelligence raises similar concerns—but with higher stakes. Unlike calculators, AI can generate writing, summarize information, solve problems, and imitate reasoning. Because AI performs cognitive tasks rather than simple computation, the modern debate is far more complex.

Defining Deskilling

Deskilling refers to the weakening or replacement of human skills through automation. In education, this may include reduced writing ability, weaker memory retention, or less persistence in solving problems independently.

Defining Cognitive Offloading

Cognitive offloading occurs when people rely on external systems to perform mental tasks. Examples include using GPS for navigation or AI tools for writing and summarizing.
