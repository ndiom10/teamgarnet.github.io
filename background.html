Background

To understand whether artificial intelligence causes deskilling in education, it is necessary to examine earlier learning tools, define core concepts, and understand why AI may differ from previous technologies.

Historical Background

Educational technology has always created debate. When calculators entered classrooms, many educators feared students would stop learning mathematics fundamentals. Public resistance was common, including protests against calculator use in elementary schools.

Later research challenged those fears. A meta-analysis of studies from the 1990s found that more than two-thirds of studies reported higher overall achievement for calculator users compared with non-calculator groups. About 75% of conceptual knowledge results favored calculator users, while two-thirds of procedural knowledge results showed no significant difference. This suggested calculators improved performance without harming core paper-and-pencil skills. Another study of 275 college-bound juniors taking SAT mathematics questions found students allowed calculators scored higher overall, especially on computational questions, while performing similarly on reasoning-based questions. Students unfamiliar with calculators gained fewer benefits, showing that tools still require human understanding and practice. These findings matter because calculators mainly assisted computation. Artificial intelligence is different because it can participate in writing, summarizing, coding, reasoning, and idea generation.

Defining Deskilling

Deskilling occurs when reliance on technological systems begins to reduce existing skills or prevents skill development. In education, this may mean weaker writing ability, lower memory retention, less independent reasoning, or fewer opportunities to develop problem-solving habits. Defining Cognitive Offloading

Cognitive offloading refers to using external tools to reduce mental load. In moderation, this can be useful. Students may save time by using tools for repetitive tasks or brainstorming. However, excessive offloading can become harmful when learners allow technology to perform thinking tasks they should still practice themselves. Defining Skill Erosion

Skill erosion is the gradual loss of previously developed cognitive or practical skills because they are not regularly used. Unlike deskilling, which describes the immediate weakening of ability, skill erosion refers to the long-term decline that may happen after repeated dependence on outside tools.

Within education, this could affect critical thinking, creativity, writing fluency, memory, judgment, and intellectual confidence. [oai_citation:4‡Garnet AI & Humanity Meta-Study Research Paper (1).pdf](sediment://file_00000000cc6871fdb45b54d2c0003c90)

Why AI Is Different

Earlier tools like calculators helped students complete narrow tasks. AI tools can now generate essays, explain readings, solve equations, summarize chapters, write code, and imitate expert language. Because AI can perform broader cognitive labor, the historical success of calculators does not automatically guarantee similar outcomes for AI.

The central modern question is whether AI simply redistributes skills more efficiently—or whether it weakens the development of abilities students still need.
